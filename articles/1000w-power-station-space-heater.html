How Long Will a 1000W Power Station Run a Space Heater?

The honest answer nobody wants to hear. Plus the $45 alternative that actually works.

M
By Mike
Updated January 2026 11 min read
⚡ Quick Answer

About 1 hour. A typical 1,500W space heater will drain a 1,000Wh power station in roughly 40–50 minutes. On low (750W), you'll get about 1 hour 10 minutes. That's it.

Space heaters are the worst possible use case for portable power stations. But there ARE alternatives that work. Keep reading.

The Brutal Math

Space heaters are simple. They turn electricity into heat. No compressor cycling. No startup surges. Just a constant, massive draw of power, all the time, until you turn them off.

A standard space heater has two settings:

  • Low: 750 watts. Constant.
  • High: 1,500 watts. Constant.

That's it. No cycling. No breaks. 750 or 1,500 watts, every single second it's on.

Now the math:

Power Station Capacity Heater on LOW
(750W)		 Heater on HIGH
(1,500W)
Jackery 500 (518Wh) 518Wh 35 min ❌ Can't run it
Bluetti AC70 (768Wh) 768Wh 52 min ❌ Can't run it
EcoFlow Delta 2 (1,024Wh) 1,024Wh 1 hr 10 min 35 min
Bluetti AC200L (2,048Wh) 2,048Wh 2 hr 20 min 1 hr 10 min

Read that table again. Really sit with it.

The EcoFlow Delta 2 — my favorite power station, the one I recommend for almost everything — runs a space heater on LOW for one hour and ten minutes. On HIGH? Thirty-five minutes.

The Jackery 500? Can't even run a 1,500W heater. Its inverter maxes at 500W continuous. The heater won't even turn on.

Even the massive Bluetti AC200L — a $1,400 station with 2,048Wh — gives you about two hours on low. That's $700 per hour of heat. You could burn the money directly and stay warmer.

⚠️ The Hard Truth

Portable power stations are not designed for heating. Space heaters are the most power-hungry common household appliance. They turn your $800 battery into a one-hour hand warmer.

This isn't a flaw in the power station. It's physics. Converting electricity to heat requires enormous amounts of energy. There's no hack, no trick, no workaround that changes this math.

Why Heating Is the Worst Use Case

To understand why, compare a space heater to other things people run on power stations:

Device Watts Runtime on 1,000Wh
CPAP machine (no heat) 25W 34 hours
LED lamp 9W 94 hours
Phone charging 15W 56 hours
Chest freezer 48W avg 17 hours
Wi-Fi router 12W 70 hours
Space heater (LOW) 750W 1 hr 10 min
Space heater (HIGH) 1,500W 35 min

Your CPAP runs for 34 hours. Your space heater runs for 35 minutes. Same battery. The heater draws 60 times more power than the CPAP.

This is why I get frustrated when YouTube guys say "this power station runs EVERYTHING in your house!" while skipping past the heating question. It doesn't. It runs everything except the one thing you desperately need when it's 14°F outside.

OK, So What Actually Works?

This is where the article gets useful. Because the question isn't really "how long will my power station run a heater?" The real question is "how do I stay warm during a winter blackout?"

Different question. Better answers.

Option 1: Electric Blanket ($35–60)

This is the answer. I'm putting it first because it changed everything for us.

A standard electric blanket draws 50–100 watts on high. Most of the time closer to 60–75W once it reaches temperature.

That same 1,000Wh power station that ran a space heater for 35 minutes? It runs an electric blanket for 11–14 hours. All night. Easily.

Heating Method Watts Runtime on 1,000Wh Cost
Space heater (HIGH) 1,500W 35 min $25–50
Space heater (LOW) 750W 1 hr 10 min $25–50
Electric blanket (HIGH) 70W 12 hours $35–60
Electric blanket (LOW) 40W 21 hours $35–60
Heated mattress pad 60W 14 hours $40–80

Look at that difference. 35 minutes vs 12 hours. Same battery. Same cost. The electric blanket doesn't heat the room — it heats you. Which is all that matters at 2 AM when you're trying to sleep.

January 2026. Power goes out. Instead of plugging in the space heater, I plugged in two electric blankets — one for our bed, one for the guest room where dad was staying.

Total draw: 130 watts. Both blankets on medium.

EcoFlow Delta 2 runtime at 130W: about 6.5 hours. Enough to get through the night. Woke up warm. Battery at 11%.

Option 2: Heated Vest or Jacket ($40–80)

This sounds ridiculous until you try it.

Heated vests have small battery packs built in. They draw zero watts from your power station. The built-in battery lasts 4–8 hours depending on the heat setting. When it dies, you recharge it from your power station's USB port — about 15W for 2 hours.

So for the cost of 30Wh (charging the vest battery), you get 6+ hours of personal warmth. Compare that to a space heater burning 750Wh for the same 6 hours. That's a 25x efficiency gain.

I bought a heated vest on Amazon last December. $52. Three heat zones — back, chest, collar. I wore it around the house during the January outage. Felt like being hugged by a warm person who doesn't talk. Perfect.

Sarah bought one the next day. Did not consult me. Just showed up in a heated vest. "These are good." That's all she said.

Option 3: Propane Heater (Indoor-Safe Models)

This is where I'll get emails. So let me be specific.

There are propane heaters rated for indoor use. The Mr. Heater Buddy is the most common. It runs on small propane canisters, produces 4,000–9,000 BTU of heat, and has a built-in oxygen depletion sensor that shuts it off if oxygen levels drop.

It uses zero electricity. Your power station doesn't care.

⚠️ Propane Safety — Read This

Only use propane heaters explicitly rated for indoor use. Not outdoor patio heaters. Not camping stoves. Not construction heaters.

Even with indoor-rated models:

  • Crack a window 1 inch for ventilation
  • Keep a CO detector in the room (battery-powered)
  • Never sleep with it running
  • Keep it away from curtains, furniture, pets
  • Place on a flat, hard surface — never carpet

I use a Mr. Heater Buddy. I follow every rule above. I still turn it off before sleeping and switch to the electric blanket on the power station for overnight warmth.

The Mr. Heater Buddy costs about $80. A 1-pound propane canister ($4) lasts 3–5 hours. It heats a medium room to comfortable in about 15 minutes.

During our January outage, I ran the Buddy for 2 hours in the living room. Brought the room from 52°F to 68°F. Then shut it off, closed the door, and the room held 60°F for another 3 hours.

Heat the room when you're awake. Electric blanket when you sleep. Power station stays charged for the important stuff — fridge, CPAP, phones, lights.

Option 4: Sleeping Bag Strategy

No electricity needed. Zero watts.

A decent 30°F sleeping bag costs $40–80. Put it on your bed. Sleep in it. You'll be warm at any indoor temperature above 40°F, which your house won't drop below for at least 12–24 hours in most climates.

This is the fallback. The "all else fails" plan. And honestly? It works better than most people expect.

Dad slept in a sleeping bag during a camping trip at age 64. Complained the entire time. Slept nine hours straight. Woke up warm. Complained again while eating breakfast.

The "One Room" Strategy (This Is What We Do)

Here's our actual winter blackout plan. Developed after three outages and one very educational argument about thermodynamics at midnight.

Mike's Winter Blackout Protocol:

Step 1: Pick one room. Smallest bedroom or living room. Close all doors.

Step 2: Everyone goes in that room. People and pets. Body heat matters — four humans and a dog in a closed room raise the temperature by 3–5°F just by existing.

Step 3: If awake — run the Mr. Heater Buddy for 1–2 hours. Heat the room to comfortable. Turn it off.

Step 4: If sleeping — electric blankets plugged into the power station. One per bed. 60–70W each.

Step 5: Power station handles: electric blankets + phone charging + one LED light + Wi-Fi router. Total draw: ~100–120W. Runtime on 1,000Wh: 7–8 hours. Enough for a full night.

Step 6: Morning — recharge the power station from solar (if available) or car (12V adapter, takes 3–4 hours). Repeat the next night if needed.

This plan uses zero space-heater watts from the power station. Zero. The propane handles room heating. The electric blankets handle sleeping. The power station handles everything else.

We've used this plan twice. Both times it worked perfectly. Both times Sarah fell asleep before me because her blanket was on high and mine was on medium. I've since switched to high. Marriage is about compromise.

What About Those Tiny Ceramic Heaters?

People ask about small personal ceramic heaters. The kind you put under your desk at work.

They draw 200–400W. Better than a full-size space heater, sure. On a 1,000Wh station, a 200W ceramic heater runs for about 4 hours. A 400W one for about 2 hours.

Better than 35 minutes. Still worse than an electric blanket.

The problem: a 200W ceramic heater barely heats a bathroom. It's designed to warm your feet under a desk, not keep a family alive during a winter blackout. You'd need to sit right next to it, and even then, the rest of you is cold.

An electric blanket at 70W wraps your entire body in warmth for 12 hours. The ceramic heater warms your shins for 4 hours. The blanket wins. It's not close.

💡 If You Must Use a Space Heater

Use it strategically. Don't run it continuously.

  • Close off one small room
  • Run the heater on LOW for 20–30 minutes to warm the room
  • Turn it off. The room holds heat for 1–2 hours
  • When it cools, run another 20 minutes
  • You'll use 250–375Wh per hour of warmth instead of 750Wh

It's not great. But it stretches a 1,000Wh station from 1 hour to about 2.5–3 hours of usable warmth.

FAQ — Quick Answers

Can a power station run an oil-filled radiator heater?

Same problem. Oil-filled radiators draw 600–1,500W. They're more efficient at distributing heat evenly, but they consume the same electricity. A 1,000Wh station runs a 600W oil radiator for about 1.5 hours. Better than a 1,500W ceramic heater, but still short.

What about a heat pump or mini-split?

Heat pumps are 2–3x more efficient than resistive heaters. A portable heat pump (if you can find one) draws 300–500W and produces the equivalent of 900–1,500W of heat. On a 1,000Wh station, that's 2–3 hours. Better, but still not all night. And portable heat pumps cost $300–600.

Can solar panels keep up with a space heater?

No. A 200W solar panel produces about 100–160W in good conditions. A space heater on low draws 750W. You'd need 5–7 solar panels running simultaneously to keep up. That's $750–1,500 in panels. For one space heater. This is not a viable strategy.

What temperature will my house drop to?

Depends on insulation, outside temp, and house size. General rule: a well-insulated house loses about 1–2°F per hour without heat in winter. If it's 68°F when power goes out and 20°F outside, your house hits about 50°F in 8–10 hours. Uncomfortable but survivable with blankets.

Is a gas generator better for heating?

Yes — if you're committed to running a space heater. A 3,500W gas generator runs a 1,500W space heater for 8+ hours on a tank of gas. But it runs outdoors, makes noise, needs ventilation, and costs $400–800. For dedicated winter heating backup, a gas generator beats a power station every time. Mike's Verdict

I'll be direct: don't buy a power station to run a space heater. It's the wrong tool for the job. Like using a sports car to haul lumber. It'll technically work for about 12 minutes and then you'll need a new plan.

Instead: buy a power station for everything EXCEPT heating — fridge, CPAP, lights, phones, router. Then handle heating separately with electric blankets (plugged into the power station at a fraction of the draw), a propane buddy heater (zero electricity), and sleeping bags (zero everything).

Total cost for the heating kit: about $200. Runtime on your power station: all night. Warmth level: genuinely comfortable.
