Why Fridges Are Tricky (The Compressor Problem)

Here's what confuses everyone. The label on your fridge says something like "120V, 5A, 600W."

600 watts?! Your 500Wh power station can barely run it for an hour!

Except... no. That 600W number is the startup surge. The compressor needs a big jolt of power to start. For about half a second. Then it drops to 100–200W running. Then it cycles off completely. Then it sits there drawing 1–3W doing nothing.

The actual average draw? Usually 50–80W for a full-size fridge. Maybe 40–60W for a chest freezer.

The key numbers:

Startup surge: 400–1,200W (lasts 0.5–2 seconds)
Running watts: 100–200W (while compressor is on)
Average watts: 50–80W (across the full cycle)

Your power station needs to handle the surge — but only pays for the average.

This is why a 500W power station can run most fridges — even though the label says 600W. The surge is brief, and modern stations handle brief surges above their rated watts.

But not all of them. More on that in a minute.

The Startup Surge Problem (And How to Solve It)

Here's where people get burned. Literally.

They buy a power station rated at 300W continuous. Plug in their fridge. The compressor tries to start, demands 800W for half a second, and the power station goes CLICK — overload protection. Shuts down.

Fridge doesn't start. Food gets warm. Owner writes an angry Amazon review.

Not the station's fault. Not the fridge's fault. Just a mismatch.

⚠️ The Rule For Fridges

Your power station's surge rating must exceed your fridge's startup surge. Period.

Most full-size fridges surge at 800–1,200W. Most chest freezers at 400–700W. Most mini fridges at 200–400W.

If your power station's surge rating is below those numbers, it won't start the compressor. The station will shut off to protect itself.

💡 How To Check Your Fridge's Surge

Look at the sticker inside your fridge — usually on the side wall or back. It'll list amps and volts. Multiply them: Amps × Volts = Watts.

That number is usually the running watts, not the surge. Multiply by 3 for a rough surge estimate.

Or just plug in a Kill-A-Watt meter ($20 on Amazon) and watch the startup spike. That's the real number.

The 6 Things That Kill Your Runtime

1. Opening the Door

Every time you open the fridge, cold air falls out and warm air rushes in. The compressor has to work harder. Runtime drops.

During a blackout, treat your fridge like a vault. Open it only when you absolutely need something. Get in, grab it, close it. No standing there staring at leftovers wondering what to eat. I see you, Sarah.

2. Room Temperature

A fridge in a 95°F garage works way harder than one in a 70°F kitchen. In my testing, the same chest freezer drew 48W average in the house and 67W average in the garage during summer. That's a 40% difference.

If you have a choice during an outage — keep the power station-fed fridge in the coolest room possible.

3. How Full It Is

A full fridge holds cold better than an empty one. All that frozen food acts like a giant ice pack. A full freezer will stay cold for hours even without power.

An empty freezer warms up fast. If yours is half-empty, fill the space with water bottles. Freeze them before storm season. They'll keep the freezer cold longer and reduce compressor cycling.

💡 The Water Bottle Trick

Fill empty space in your freezer with water bottles. They freeze solid and act as thermal mass. During an outage, your compressor cycles less because the frozen bottles keep everything cold.

In my test, a full freezer ran 22% longer on the same battery compared to a half-full one. Free runtime. Just add water.

4. Fridge Age and Efficiency

Modern Energy Star fridges draw 40–60% less than models from 15+ years ago. If your fridge is from 2008, it's probably drawing twice what a new one would.

I'm not saying buy a new fridge for your power station. But know that old fridge = shorter runtime.

5. Temperature Setting

Your fridge is probably set to 37°F. Your freezer to 0°F. During a blackout, consider bumping the fridge up to 40°F (still safe per FDA) and the freezer to 5°F. The compressor will cycle less. You'll gain 1–2 hours of runtime.

Small change. Real difference.

6. Frost Buildup

If your chest freezer has half an inch of frost on the walls, the compressor works harder. Defrost before storm season. It takes an afternoon and saves you hours of runtime when it matters.

Fridge OR Freezer — Which to Save?

If you only have one power station and two things to keep cold, which gets the plug?

The freezer. Almost always.

Here's why:

  • A closed freezer full of food stays safe for 48 hours WITHOUT power
  • A closed fridge only stays safe for 4 hours without power
  • BUT — a freezer draws less power on average than a fridge
  • The food in your freezer is usually worth more ($200–500 in meat)
  • Fridge items (condiments, leftovers) are cheaper to replace

Wait. If the freezer stays safe for 48 hours unplugged, why plug it in at all?

Because that 48-hour number assumes the freezer is completely full and you never open it. Reality? Your freezer is three-quarters full. Someone opened it to check. Now it's maybe 24 hours. Then 18. Then you're gambling.

My strategy during our March 2025 outage:

Hours 1–4: Powered the chest freezer. Fridge stayed closed — still in the safe zone.
Hour 4: Swapped the plug to the fridge for 2 hours. Let it cool back down.
Hour 6: Swapped back to the freezer.

Rotated every 2–4 hours. Both stayed safe. Power came back at hour 8. Nothing lost.

This rotation strategy works because neither the fridge nor the freezer needs continuous power. They just need enough to keep the temperature in the safe zone. A few hours on, a few hours off. The insulation does the rest.

What About a Full-Size Fridge AND Freezer at the Same Time?

Possible. But you need the math to work.

Full-size fridge (72W avg) + chest freezer (48W avg) = 120W average. On a 1,000Wh station, that's about 7 hours. On a 2,000Wh station, 14 hours.

But here's the catch — both compressors might start at the same time. Your fridge surges 800W and your freezer surges 550W. That's 1,350W simultaneously. The EcoFlow Delta 2 handles it (2,700W surge). A smaller station might not.

⚠️ Two Fridges = Check Your Surge

If you're running two refrigeration appliances simultaneously, add both startup surges together. Your power station's surge rating must exceed that total. Otherwise one compressor will trip the overload protection when both try to start at once.

Safest approach: plug them into a power strip with a delay timer, or just manually stagger them — plug the fridge in first, wait for the compressor to settle, then plug in the freezer.

FAQ — Quick Answers

Will a 500Wh station run my full-size fridge?

Yes — IF the station's surge rating exceeds ~1,000W. It'll run the fridge for about 5–7 hours. Enough for a short outage. Not enough for overnight.

Can I run a fridge on solar power alone?

During daylight, yes. A 200W solar panel produces roughly 100–160W in good sun. That's enough to run a fridge AND recharge the battery slowly. At night, the battery takes over. With a 1,000Wh station and a 200W panel, you can run a fridge indefinitely.

What about an inverter generator instead?

Works great. A 2,000W inverter generator handles any fridge easily. But it needs gas, makes noise, produces fumes, and can't run indoors. If you want silent, fume-free indoor backup — power station. Full comparison in my Power Station vs Generator article.

My fridge keeps tripping the power station. What's wrong?

Your station's surge rating is too low for your fridge's startup surge. Solutions: (1) buy a station with higher surge rating, (2) try a "soft start" plug adapter — they reduce startup surge by 50–70%, or (3) try a different, newer fridge. Older compressors surge harder.

Should I unplug the fridge between cycles to save power?

No. The fridge's thermostat already does this. The compressor cycles on and off automatically. Unplugging it manually just means it warms up more between cycles and the compressor works harder when you plug it back in. Let the fridge do its job.
