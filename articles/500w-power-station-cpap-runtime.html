The Math (Simple Version)

Before the test results — here's how the math works. It's simpler than you think.

The formula:
Battery (Wh) ÷ CPAP Draw (W) × 0.85 = Runtime (hours)
The 0.85 accounts for inverter efficiency loss. Every power station wastes ~15% converting DC to AC.

A typical CPAP draws between 25–60 watts without heated humidifier. With the heater on, it jumps to 80–150 watts.

That's a HUGE range. Which is why forum answers are all over the place. Someone says "my CPAP ran all night!" and someone else says "mine died in three hours."

Both are right. One had the heater off. One had it cranked to max.

The single biggest factor: heated humidifier. On or off. That one setting changes your runtime by 2–3x. Everything else is secondary.

So... Can You Turn the Heater Off?

This is the question everyone asks next. And the answer is: probably yes.

I asked dad to try one night without the heated humidifier. He was skeptical. He's been using it for four years.

Result: he slept fine. His throat was a little dry in the morning. He said it was "not great, not terrible." (Dad is not dramatic.)

Here's what sleep doctors generally recommend for CPAP without heated humidifier:

  • Use a CPAP-specific moisture pad on your face
  • Run a standalone cool-mist humidifier nearby (draws ~30W — still way less than a CPAP heater)
  • Keep water by the bed for dry mouth
  • It's one night. You'll survive. Your battery will too.

The DC Adapter Trick (Bonus 30% Runtime)

This is the thing nobody talks about and it drives me crazy.

When you plug your CPAP into the AC outlet of a power station, the power goes through an inverter. DC battery → AC outlet → CPAP's own power brick → back to DC inside the CPAP.

That's two conversions. Each one wastes energy. You lose about 15–20% of your battery to heat and inefficiency.

But most modern CPAPs can run on DC power directly. ResMed sells a DC converter cable (about $30). Plug it into the 12V car outlet on your power station.

Result?

DC adapter = 25–35% more runtime.

That Jackery 500 that gave 14 hours on AC? With a DC adapter: ~18 hours.
That 3-hour heater-on runtime? Becomes ~4 hours.

A $30 cable buys you hours of breathing. Best money you'll spend.

I bought the ResMed DC/DC converter on Amazon. $28. Took two days to arrive. Plugged it into the 12V port on the Jackery. Gained almost four extra hours with heater off.

Dad now keeps one in his travel bag permanently.

What About BiPAP and APAP?

Quick note because I get this email at least twice a week.

APAP (auto-adjusting CPAP) — draws roughly the same as a standard CPAP. Same runtimes. The motor adjusts pressure but doesn't use significantly more power.

BiPAP — draws more. Typically 40–60W without heater, 100–180W with heater. If you use a BiPAP, add about 30% to all the runtimes above. Meaning subtract 30% from the hours.

A 500Wh station with a BiPAP and heater on? Maybe 2.5 hours. Not enough. Go 1,000Wh minimum.

The Settings That Kill Your Battery

Four things eat your power station alive. In order of damage:

1. Heated Humidifier (the big one)

We covered this. Heater off = 2–3x more runtime. Period. In an emergency, turn it off.

2. Heated Tube

Some CPAPs have a heated tube to prevent rainout (condensation dripping on your face). It draws 15–25W extra. That's 3–4 hours of runtime lost on a 500Wh station. Turn it off during a blackout.

3. High Pressure Settings

A CPAP at pressure 5 draws less than one at pressure 20. You can't change your prescription — don't mess with your pressure. But know that higher pressure = shorter runtime. It's physics.

4. Mask Leak

This one surprises people. If your mask leaks, the CPAP motor works harder to maintain pressure. Harder motor = more watts = shorter runtime.

A well-fitted mask isn't just more comfortable. It literally saves battery.

Dad had a mask that was two years old. Seal was worn. I bought him a new one — $35 on Amazon. His CPAP's power draw dropped from 32W to 24W average. That's a 25% improvement. From a $35 mask.

💡 Battery-Saving CPAP Checklist
  • Turn heated humidifier OFF
  • Turn heated tube OFF
  • Use DC adapter instead of AC outlet
  • Check mask seal — replace if worn
  • Start with power station at 100%

Do all five and a 500Wh station can last 18+ hours. That's two full nights of breathing.

FAQ — Quick Answers

Can I use my CPAP while the power station charges?

Yes — most modern stations support pass-through charging. The EcoFlow Delta 2 does. The Jackery 300 Plus does. Check your manual, but in most cases you can charge from solar during the day and run CPAP at night without unplugging anything.

Will the power station's fan wake me up?

Probably not. At 25–30W draw (CPAP without heater), most stations don't even turn their fan on. The load is too small. The Jackery 500 was completely silent during my overnight test. The EcoFlow Delta 2 clicked its fan on once for about 30 seconds around hour six. Dad didn't notice.

What if my CPAP needs a specific voltage?

Most CPAPs run on standard 110V/120V AC — exactly what power stations output. If you're using the DC adapter, check that your cable matches the voltage. ResMed machines use 24V DC. The wrong cable can damage the machine. Stick with official ResMed or Philips DC adapters.

Can a cheap Amazon power station work?

It can. But cheap stations often have "dirty" sine waves (modified sine wave inverters). Some CPAPs don't care. Some get weird — buzzing, error codes, or just refusing to start. Every station I recommend uses pure sine wave inverters. For medical equipment, don't gamble on a $79 no-name brand.

What about oxygen concentrators?

Different beast. Portable oxygen concentrators draw 100–300W depending on flow rate. A 500Wh station might run a low-flow concentrator for 2–4 hours. For oxygen concentrators, go 1,000Wh minimum. I'm working on a separate article for this — email me if you need help now.
