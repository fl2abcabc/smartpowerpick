Best Portable Power Stations 2026: Tested and Compared

I've tested 14 power stations over the past two years. Here's what's worth your money heading into 2026.

By Mike Updated January 2026 18 min read
⚡
All four power stations tested side by side — photo coming soon

⚡ Short on Time? Here's the Quick Answer

No time for my camping stories? I get it. Here's the cheat sheet.

Pick Model Price Best For
🏆 Best Overall EcoFlow Delta 2 $999 Remote work, camping, home backup
💰 Best Budget Jackery Explorer 300 $289 Phone charging, light camping, CPAP
⚡ Best Mid-Range Jackery Explorer 1000 $999 Weekend camping, simple reliability
🔋 Best Heavy-Duty Bluetti AC200P $1,399 Hurricane prep, off-grid, whole-home backup

Still here? Good. Pull up a chair.

I didn't plan on becoming "the power station guy."

It started two years ago. We lost power for 19 hours during a winter storm. No heat, no Wi-Fi, no coffee. Sarah wrapped herself in three blankets and gave me the look. You know the look. The one that says "fix this or I'm going to my mother's."

I ordered an EcoFlow Delta 2 that night. From my phone. Under the blankets. With 11% battery left.

That was the beginning.

Two years later, I've tested 14 different portable power stations. Some I bought. Some I returned. One I dropped in a parking lot — we'll get to that.

My garage looks like a battery showroom. Sarah calls it "Mike's expensive hobby." Rex — our lab — sleeps next to whichever unit is charging. I think he likes the warmth.

This isn't a list I copied from spec sheets. I've used every single one of these. Camping in Durango. Working from the van near Moab. Surviving two more power outages — because apparently Colorado's grid runs on hope and prayers.

Here's what I learned.

🏆 EcoFlow Delta 2 — Best Overall

📷
EcoFlow Delta 2 on my workbench — photo coming soon

How I Actually Use It

I'm writing this from my van right now. Parked outside a coffee shop in Fort Collins, but I'm not going inside. Their Wi-Fi is terrible and the coffee is $7.

My setup: MacBook Pro, portable monitor, hotspot, phone charging. All running off the Delta 2. It's 10:14 AM. Battery shows 81%. I started working at 7.

I'll be fine until 4 PM. I know this because I've done it probably 40 times now.

That's the thing about the Delta 2. It's boring. In the best way. You plug stuff in. It works. You forget about it.

Until you need it to do something crazy.

The Thanksgiving Test

Last November. Power went out at 2 PM. Turkey in the oven. Twelve people coming at 5.

Sarah didn't give me the look this time. She just pointed at the garage.

I plugged the oven into the Delta 2.

Now — let me be honest. I wasn't sure this would work. An oven pulls a lot of watts. The Delta 2 is rated for 1800W, and our oven pulls about 1650W on bake.

It worked.

For about 45 minutes. Got us to maybe 80% done on the turkey. Then the battery hit 15% and I switched to just running the lights and the fridge.

Power came back at 4:20 PM. Turkey was done by 5. Nobody knew we almost had a Thanksgiving disaster.

Except Sarah. Sarah knows everything.

The Numbers

Capacity1,024Wh
Output1,800W (surge 2,700W)
Battery TypeLiFePO4
Weight27 lbs
AC Charging0-80% in 50 minutes
Solar Input500W max
Lifespan3,000+ cycles
Price~$999

That charging speed. Read it again.

0 to 80% in 50 minutes.

This is where the Delta 2 destroys the competition. My Jackery 1000 takes about 5.5 hours to do the same thing. I've literally gone grocery shopping and come back to a Jackery still charging.

What I Love

  • Charging speed — 50 minutes to 80%
  • X-Boost handles devices up to 2200W
  • Clear display with real-time watts in/out
  • LiFePO4 — 3,000 cycle lifespan

What I Don't Love

  • 27 lbs — not fun uphill
  • App works about 85% of the time
  • Fan kicks in above 600W — noticeable

Carl's Take

Remember Carl? Retired electrician, campsite neighbor in Durango? He bought a Delta 2 three weeks after our conversation. Texted me about it.

"Thing charges faster than my phone."

Then, two days later:

"The app sucks."

Yeah, Carl. I know.

Who Should Buy the EcoFlow Delta 2

  • You work remotely and need reliable all-day power
  • You camp regularly and value fast solar charging
  • You want home backup for outages (fridge + lights + phones)
  • You don't mind spending $1,000 for peace of mind

Who Should Skip It

  • You just need phone charging — get a Jackery 300
  • You want to power your entire house — go bigger
  • You're a backpacker — 27 lbs is not backpack-friendly

If I could only keep ONE power station from my collection, it's this one.

9/10
Mike's Rating
One point off for the app. And the weight. That's two things. Math was never my strong suit.

⚡ Jackery Explorer 1000 — The Reliable Workhorse

📷
Jackery 1000 in my car trunk — its natural habitat — photo coming soon

The One That Started It All

Funny thing. The Jackery 1000 was actually my first power station. Before the Delta 2. Before the obsession. Before Sarah started calling the garage "the battery museum."

I bought it in 2022. No drama. No power outage. Just saw it at Costco, thought "that could be useful," and tossed it in the cart next to paper towels and a 48-pack of granola bars.

You know how Costco works.

First Year — Honest Take

For about eight months, it mostly sat in the garage. I'd charge it every couple of months just because the manual said I should. Rex sniffed it once. Lost interest.

Then summer came and Sarah wanted to go camping.

I threw the Jackery in the car almost as an afterthought. Didn't even read the manual properly. Figured I'd plug stuff in and hope for the best.

And you know what? That's exactly what happened.

Phone charging. Check. LED string lights around the campsite. Check. Small fan in the tent because August in Colorado is no joke. Check.

Nothing exciting. Nothing broke. Nothing caught fire. It just worked.

The Numbers

Capacity1,002Wh
Output1,000W (surge 2,000W)
Battery TypeLi-ion
Weight22 lbs
AC Charging~5.5 hours
Solar Input200W max
Lifespan500+ cycles
Price~$999

Five and a half hours charging. After using the Delta 2, going back to the Jackery feels like switching from broadband to dial-up. Remember dial-up? That sound? I'm dating myself.

What Numbers Don't Tell You

The Jackery is simple. Two buttons. One screen. No app. No Bluetooth. No firmware updates. No notifications telling me to optimize my charging schedule.

My dad is 67. He's not updating firmware. He's not troubleshooting Bluetooth. He wants to press a button and plug in his reading lamp when the power goes out.

I bought him a Jackery 1000 for Christmas. He's used it four times during outages. Called me zero times for tech support.

That's worth something.

What I Like

  • 5 lbs lighter than Delta 2 — knees notice
  • Dead simple — two buttons, no app needed
  • Tough build — 2 years of trunk life, still works
  • Weekend camping on a single charge

What I Don't Like

  • 5.5 hours AC charging — painfully slow in 2026
  • Li-ion battery — only 500 cycles vs 3,000
  • No expandable battery option
  • 200W solar max — takes all day

The Dad Test

I called my dad last week. Asked how the Jackery was doing.

"The what?"

"The orange box I gave you for Christmas."

"Oh! The power thing. Yeah. It's in the closet. Works great when we need it."

In the closet. Works great when we need it. That might be the best review a product can get.

Who Should Buy the Jackery 1000

  • You camp casually and need reliable weekend power
  • You want something simple with no learning curve
  • You're buying for parents or anyone who hates tech
  • You find a good deal — $649 on Prime Day is solid

Who Should Skip It

  • You need fast daily recharging — 5.5 hours will drive you crazy
  • You want serious home backup — maybe 15 hours of fridge, then dark
  • You care about long-term battery life — 500 vs 3,000 cycles

The Jackery 1000 is a Honda Civic. Not the fastest. Not the fanciest. But it starts every morning and doesn't ask for much.

7.5/10
Mike's Rating
At full price in 2026 — better options exist. At $649 on sale? That's the sweet spot.

🔋 Bluetti AC200P — The Monster in the Corner

📷
Bluetti AC200P — yes, Rex is sleeping next to it — photo coming soon

I need to be upfront about something. The AC200P is the power station I use the least.

It's been in my garage since March. I've fully charged it maybe six times. Tested it on a few things around the house. Ran the fridge for a day just to see if I could.

Then I put it back in the corner.

Not because it's bad. Because it's too much for my daily life. It's like owning a pickup truck in Manhattan. Impressive, sure. But where are you going to park it?

This thing weighs 60 pounds. Sixty. I don't carry it anywhere. It lives in one spot. Rex sleeps next to it sometimes. I think he thinks it's furniture.

So Why Is It On My List?

Because of Tom.

Tom lives in Cape Coral, Florida. We went to college together. He's an accountant, which is the least interesting thing about him, and he'd agree.

What's interesting about Tom is that he survived Hurricane Ian in 2022 with no power for six days. Six days. Florida. September. No AC.

I'm not going to tell you a dramatic storm story because Tom doesn't tell it that way. He's an accountant, remember. He tells it like a spreadsheet.

"Day one. Fine. Day two. Fridge stuff started going bad. Day three. Threw out everything. Day four. Found a gas station with a generator. Waited two hours in line for gas. Day five. Same. Day six. Power came back."

What he doesn't say — his wife Maria told me later — is that their daughter cried on day three because her fish died. The tank pump stopped.

After Ian, Tom bought an AC200P. No research. No comparison shopping. He called me, said "what's the biggest one," I said Bluetti AC200P, he ordered it that afternoon.

What Tom Tells Me

We talk on the phone most Sundays. Fantasy football, mostly. But since he got the Bluetti, he gives me updates like I asked for them. I didn't.

"Tested it on the fridge this weekend. Ran for 21 hours."

"Got the solar panels. Charged in about 6 hours on the lanai."

"Maria put a tablecloth on it. It's in the dining room now."

Last September, tropical storm season, they lost power for about 14 hours. Tom ran the fridge, the fish tank, two fans, and charged four phones. Used 60% of the battery.

He texted me a photo of the fish. Alive. With a thumbs up emoji. That's the whole review right there.

The Numbers

Capacity2,000Wh
Output2,000W (surge 4,800W)
Battery TypeLiFePO4
Weight60.5 lbs
AC Charging~4 hours
Solar Input700W max
Lifespan3,500+ cycles
Price~$1,399

Two thousand watt-hours. That's roughly double the Delta 2. Double the weight too. And $400 more. The question isn't whether it's powerful. It is. The question is whether YOU need that much power.

My Garage Test

Fridge test: Ran our full-size kitchen fridge for 18 hours. Tom got 21 but Florida fridges work harder in the heat. Makes sense.

Everything test: Plugged in the fridge, a lamp, the Wi-Fi router, and charged two phones simultaneously. Used about 8% per hour. Math says that's roughly 12 hours of "the power is out but you'd barely notice" living.

The microwave: Yep. It runs a microwave. 1100W, no problem. Heated up leftover pasta at 11 PM just because I could. Sarah came downstairs.

"Why are you microwaving pasta with the power on?"

"Science."

That look again.

What Works

  • Sheer capacity — 2,000Wh runs a home for hours
  • LiFePO4 — 3,500 cycles, will outlive Tom
  • 700W solar input — 5-6 hours full charge
  • Runs microwave, fridge, fans simultaneously

What Doesn't

  • 60 lbs — this is furniture, not portable
  • $1,399 — serious money
  • Overkill for camping and daily use
  • No wheels — at 60 lbs it should have wheels

Who Should Buy the Bluetti AC200P

  • You live in hurricane/storm territory (Florida, Texas, Louisiana)
  • You need to keep a fridge running during long outages
  • You have a permanent spot for it (garage, closet, dining room)
  • You think of it as home insurance, not a gadget

Who Should Skip It

  • You want to take it camping — your back will file a complaint
  • You just need phone and laptop charging — absurd overkill
  • You're on a budget — $1,399 plus panels is a real investment
8.5/10
Mike's Rating
Excellent at what it does. But "what it does" is a specific thing for specific people.

💰 Jackery Explorer 300 — Small, Cheap, Surprisingly Useful

📷
Jackery 300 next to a coffee mug for scale — photo coming soon

I almost didn't include this one. A 300Wh power station in a list with 1000Wh and 2000Wh monsters feels like bringing a bicycle to a car show.

But then I got an email.

Not from Jackery. From a reader named Diana. She lives in a third-floor apartment in Sacramento. No garage. No yard. No room for a 60-pound battery.

"I just need to keep my fish tank running and my phone charged when PG&E does their shutoff thing. Everything I read online tells me to buy a $1,000 power station. I don't have $1,000. Am I just stuck?"

No, Diana. You're not stuck.

What 300Wh Actually Gets You

iPhone 1520+ full charges
MacBook Air4 full charges
CPAP (no heater)3-4 nights
Fish tank pump (10W)25+ hours
LED string lights50+ hours
Portable fan12-15 hours
Mini projector3 movies
DJI Mini drone5 charges

Twenty-plus phone charges. From something the size of a toaster that weighs 7 pounds. Seven. Pounds. Sarah carries heavier purses.

The Numbers

Capacity293Wh
Output300W (surge 500W)
Battery TypeLi-ion
Weight7.1 lbs
AC Charging~2 hours
Solar Input65W max
Price~$289

What It WON'T Do

❌ Run a fridge — drains in 2-3 hours
❌ Power a microwave — 1000W+ vs 300W max
❌ Run a space heater — don't even try
❌ Be your whole-home backup — this is a flashlight, not a lighthouse

If you need those things, scroll up to the Delta 2 or the Bluetti. Honesty saves everybody's time.

Where It Shines

The Diana scenario. PG&E shuts off power for 36 hours because it's windy. Again. With a Jackery 300: fish alive, phone charged, laptop running for a workday. Sanity mostly preserved.

CPAP users — this is a big one. Millions of Americans use CPAP machines for sleep apnea. With the Jackery 300 and a CPAP running at 30W without the heated humidifier: two full nights plus leftover for phone charging. That's not luxury. For some people that's "I can go camping again."

Kids' soccer game. Last month I used it at a soccer tournament. Three parents asked me to charge their phones. I felt like a hero. A very small hero. But still.

Who Should Buy the Jackery 300

  • You live in an apartment — small, light, fits in a closet
  • You need CPAP backup for camping or outages
  • You want basic emergency prep without big investment
  • You go to festivals, tailgates, beach days
  • You want a first power station to see if you even use one

Who Should Skip It

  • You need to run a fridge or any large appliance
  • You want multi-day home backup
  • You work remotely and need all-day laptop power

It's $289. It weighs 7 pounds. It charges your phone 20 times. Sometimes that's all you need.

8/10

🎯 Which One Should YOU Buy?

I've been doing this long enough to know that nobody cares about watt-hours until they need them. So forget the specs for a minute. Find yourself below.

"I just don't want my phone to die"

Beach. Festival. Camping once a year. You just want your phone charged and maybe a speaker.

→ Jackery 300. $289. Done. Don't overthink it.

"I camp most weekends with my family"

Coffee maker. Fan in the tent. Phones, tablets, lights, mini fridge.

→ Jackery 1000 if you want simple. EcoFlow Delta 2 if you want fast charging. Same price. Different personalities.

"I work from my laptop and can't lose power"

Zoom calls. Deadlines. Clients who don't care that your power is out.

→ EcoFlow Delta 2. No contest. I write on mine right now.

"Hurricane season scares me"

Gulf Coast. Atlantic coast. You remember what six days without power felt like. The fridge. The heat. The darkness.

→ Bluetti AC200P. Yes, $1,399. You know what's also expensive? Throwing out $400 of food from your fridge. Twice.

"I use a CPAP machine"

You want to camp. You want to sleep safely during outages. You need your CPAP every night.

→ Weekend trips: Jackery 300 (2 nights). Longer trips: EcoFlow Delta 2 (5-6 nights, add solar for indefinite).

"I live in an apartment — no gas generator allowed"

No garage. No yard. HOA rules. Carbon monoxide risk indoors. 73 people died from CO poisoning during Texas Winter Storm Uri.

→ Any portable power station. Silent. No fumes. Jackery 300 for basics. Delta 2 if the fridge matters.

"I run a small business on the go"

Food truck. Farm stand. Market booth. Photography. DJ. A dead battery means lost income.

→ EcoFlow Delta 2 for moderate needs. Bluetti AC200P for heavy equipment. Write it off as a business expense.

"California keeps shutting off my power"

PSPS. Public Safety Power Shutoffs. PG&E sends a text and your lights go out for 48 hours. Again.

→ EcoFlow Delta 2 + 220W solar panel. California sun charges it in about 3 hours. Free electricity from the thing causing the shutoffs — wind and sun.

"I'm on a tight budget but want SOMETHING"

Better to have a small power station than no power station. Period.

→ Jackery 300 at $289. Or wait for Prime Day — it's been as low as $189. Set a price alert on CamelCamelCamel.

One More Thing

A year ago I got an email from a nurse in Houston. She works night shifts. After the 2021 freeze, she bought an EcoFlow Delta 2.

She didn't use it for a year. Kept it charged in the closet. Her husband joked about their "$1,000 paperweight."

Then last winter, a storm knocked out power at 6 AM. She'd just gotten home from a 12-hour shift. Her CPAP was dead. The house was getting cold. Her phone was at 8%.

She plugged everything into the Delta 2. CPAP, phone, a space heater on low. Slept six hours. Woke up to the power still out but her phone charged, her house warm enough, and her husband making coffee with their camping percolator plugged into the Delta 2.

"Best paperweight we ever bought," he said.

She told me I could share the story. So I did.

Final Word

You don't buy a power station because it's exciting. You buy it because the alternative is worse.

Cold house. Dead phone. Spoiled food. Missed work. Scared kids. Dead fish.

Pick your scenario. Pick your station. Charge it. Put it in the closet.

Hope you never need it.

But when you do — you'll be the person everyone on your street comes to.

Trust me. I'm that person on my street now. It's not a bad reputation to have.
