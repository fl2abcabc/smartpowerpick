How to Choose the Right Size Portable Power Station

Updated: January 2025 · 8 min read

The biggest mistake people make when buying a portable power station is getting the wrong size — either too small (and running out of power) or too large (wasting money on capacity they never use). This guide gives you a simple method to calculate exactly what size you need.

Understanding Watts vs Watt-Hours

Before choosing a size, you need to understand two key measurements:

Watts (W) measure power — how much electricity a device needs at any given moment. Think of it like the speed of water flowing through a pipe. A phone charger uses about 10W. A portable fridge uses about 50W. An electric kettle uses about 1500W.

Watt-hours (Wh) measure capacity — how much total electricity a power station can store. Think of it like the size of the water tank. A 500Wh power station holds 500 watt-hours of energy.

Runtime (hours) = Battery Capacity (Wh) ÷ Device Power (W)

For example: A 1000Wh power station running a 50W portable fridge will last approximately 1000 ÷ 50 = 20 hours.

💡 Important Note

Real-world runtime is typically 15-20% less than the calculated number due to power conversion losses and battery management. Always add a 20% buffer to your calculations.

How to Calculate Your Needs

Step 1: List Your Devices

Write down every device you plan to use with your power station. Include everything — even small things like phone chargers add up.

Step 2: Find Their Wattage

Check the label on your charger or device for the wattage. If it only shows amps and volts, multiply them together: Amps × Volts = Watts.

Step 3: Estimate Daily Hours

How many hours per day will each device run? A phone might charge for 2 hours, but a fridge runs 24 hours.

Step 4: Calculate Total Daily Wh

Total Daily Wh = (Device1 W × Hours) + (Device2 W × Hours) + ...

Step 5: Add 20% Buffer

Recommended Capacity = Total Daily Wh × 1.2

📱 Example Calculation: Weekend Camping for Two

Devices:

  • 2 × Smartphone charges: 2 × 20W × 2h = 80 Wh
  • 1 × LED light: 10W × 6h = 60 Wh
  • 1 × Portable fan: 20W × 6h = 120 Wh
  • 1 × Laptop: 60W × 2h = 120 Wh

Total: 380 Wh per day

With 20% buffer: 380 × 1.2 = 456 Wh

For a 2-day trip: 456 × 2 = 912 Wh

👉 A 1000Wh power station would be perfect for this scenario.

Power Station Size Categories

🔋 Small: 200-500Wh ($150-$400)

Best for: Day trips, phone and camera charging, minimalist camping

Can power: Smartphones (20-40 charges), laptops (3-5 charges), LED lights, small electronics

Cannot power: Fridges for extended time, high-wattage appliances, electric cooking

Weight: 5-15 lbs — easy to carry in a backpack

🔋🔋 Medium: 500-1000Wh ($400-$900)

Best for: Weekend camping, small families, CPAP users, remote work

Can power: Everything above plus portable fridge (12-20 hours), CPAP (1-2 nights), mini projector, portable fan

Cannot power: High-wattage cooking, air conditioners, power tools

Weight: 15-30 lbs — car camping friendly

🔋🔋🔋 Large: 1000-2000Wh ($800-$2,000)

Best for: Extended trips, families, RV camping, home backup

Can power: Everything above plus fridge (24-48 hours), electric kettle, small microwave, multiple devices simultaneously

Weight: 25-50 lbs — needs to stay near the car

🔋🔋🔋🔋 Extra Large: 2000Wh+ ($1,500-$4,000+)

Best for: Large groups, extended off-grid living, serious home backup

Can power: Nearly everything including high-wattage devices, multiple fridges, power tools

Weight: 50-80 lbs — usually has wheels

Pro Tips for Choosing the Right Size

1. Always Size Up

If your calculation puts you between two sizes, always go up. You will inevitably want to charge devices you did not plan for. Running a power station at 50-70% capacity is also better for long-term battery health than constantly draining it to zero.

2. Check Peak Wattage, Not Just Capacity

A 1000Wh power station with only 500W output cannot run a 600W blender, regardless of how much capacity it has. Make sure the output watts exceed your highest-wattage device by at least 20%.

3. Consider Solar Charging

If you plan to camp for more than 2 days, solar panels can extend your power station indefinitely. A smaller power station plus a solar panel can be more practical and cheaper than buying a massive station.

4. Think About Future Needs

Will you get a portable fridge next year? Start taking longer trips? Buy a larger power station now rather than upgrading later. Some brands like Jackery and EcoFlow offer expandable systems that let you add battery capacity over time.

5. Weight Matters More Than You Think

A 60-lb power station sounds manageable until you have to carry it from the parking lot to your campsite. Consider your typical setup and how far you need to transport your gear.

Frequently Asked Questions

What size power station do I need for a CPAP machine?

Most CPAP machines draw 30-60W. For one night (8 hours), you need 240-480Wh. For a weekend (2 nights), you need 480-960Wh. We recommend at least a 500Wh power station for single-night use, or 1000Wh for multi-night camping. Turn off the heated humidifier to significantly reduce power consumption.

Can a portable power station run a full-size refrigerator?

A full-size home refrigerator uses about 100-200W (with compressor cycling on and off). A 2000Wh power station could run it for roughly 10-15 hours. This is enough for a short power outage but not for extended use. For camping, use a 12V portable fridge instead — they use much less power.

How many times can a power station charge my phone?

A typical smartphone battery is about 15-20Wh. Divide your power station capacity by 20 to get a rough estimate. A 500Wh station can charge a phone about 25 times. A 1000Wh station about 50 times. Account for about 15% conversion loss.

Is it better to buy one large power station or two smaller ones?

One large unit is usually more cost-effective per Wh and easier to manage. However, two smaller units give you redundancy — if one fails, you still have backup power. Two smaller units are also easier to carry. For most people, one right-sized unit is the best choice.

What if I need more power in the future?

Consider brands that offer expandable battery systems, like the Jackery Explorer 1000 Plus or EcoFlow DELTA series. These let you add extra battery packs later without buying a completely new unit. It costs more upfront but saves money long-term if your needs grow.
