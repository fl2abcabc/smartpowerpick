What a Power Station Actually Does (Plain English)

Skip this if you already know. But a surprising number of people email me asking "wait, is this just a big battery?"

Yes. That's exactly what it is.

A portable power station is a rechargeable battery with outlets on it. Regular outlets — the same ones on your wall. Plus USB ports. Plus sometimes a car-style 12V outlet.

You charge it from your wall. Or your car. Or solar panels. Then when you need power — anywhere, anytime — you press a button and plug stuff in.

That's it. No gas. No fumes. No noise. No maintenance.

Think of it this way: A power bank charges your phone. A power station charges your phone, your laptop, your mini fridge, your CPAP machine, your TV, your fan, your heated blanket, and your electric kettle. All at the same time. In silence.

Now — is that worth $300 to $2,000? Let's find out.

When a Power Station is 100% Worth It

✅ Absolutely Worth It If You:
  • Live anywhere with power outages (so... everywhere)
  • Camp, RV, tailgate, or spend time outdoors
  • Work remotely from anywhere other than your desk
  • Use a CPAP, oxygen concentrator, or medical device
  • Have a chest freezer full of expensive food
  • Live in an apartment (can't use a generator)
  • Want emergency prep without the hassle of gas generators
  • Have kids — quiet power at 2 AM matters
  • Value convenience (one button, no maintenance, ever)

When a Power Station is NOT Worth It (Being Honest)

I sell nothing on this site. I have no reason to lie. So here's when you should save your money:

❌ Skip It If You:
  • Literally never lose power and never leave your house
  • Need to run central AC, a well pump, or a whole house — get a generator or whole-home battery instead
  • Are on a very tight budget and $200 is a real stretch right now
  • Already have a generator and you're happy with it
  • Think it will replace your home's electrical grid — it won't

I got an email from a guy in Phoenix. His power goes out maybe once every three years. He doesn't camp. Doesn't travel. Works from a desk in his house.

The Biggest Mistakes People Make

Mistake #1: Buying Too Small

A 300Wh power station sounds great until you realize it runs a mini fridge for about three hours. If you're buying for emergencies, go 1,000Wh minimum. I've said this before and I'll keep saying it.

My rule of thumb: Take what you think you need. Double it. That's your actual number. Batteries drain faster than spec sheets suggest — especially in cold weather.

Mistake #2: Buying Too Big

A 2,000Wh unit is amazing — but it weighs 50 pounds. If you're a solo camper or a 130-pound woman who needs to carry it, that's not practical. Be honest about what you'll actually lift.

Mistake #3: Ignoring Watts vs Watt-Hours

Watts = how much power it can deliver at once. Watt-hours = how long it lasts.

A 1,000W / 1,000Wh station can run a 1,000-watt microwave for about one hour. Or a 100-watt fan for about ten hours. Or a 50-watt laptop for about twenty hours.

People buy a 500Wh station and try to run a space heater. A space heater draws 1,500W. The station can't even turn it on — and if it could, it'd last 20 minutes.

Know your numbers. Or email me and I'll help.

Mistake #4: Waiting for a Blackout to Test It

Charge it. Test it. Run your fridge on it for an afternoon. See how long your CPAP actually lasts. Know BEFORE the emergency — not during.

My neighbor Frank bought one after the same storm that killed his freezer food. Great. But he didn't charge it. Three weeks later — another outage. Dead battery. $300 in steak, gone again.

Frank has learned. Slowly.
